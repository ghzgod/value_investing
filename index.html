    Field guide

    Value investing, honestly

    The idea. A company has a true worth – its intrinsic value – built from fundamentals like earnings and growth. Its market price swings around with public sentiment. When price falls well below intrinsic value, the stock trades “at a discount.” Buy the discount, the theory goes, and profit as the price recovers.

    How we measure it. This page uses Benjamin Graham's revised formula – the one from the video:

    V = EPS · (8.5 + 2g) · 4.4 / Y

    • EPS – trailing earnings per share
    • g – expected annual growth rate (%)
    • 8.5 – the P/E Graham assigned a no-growth company
    • 4.4 – the AAA corporate bond yield of Graham's era
    • Y – today's AAA bond yield

    Compare V to the live price. Trading below it? Discounted. Above it? Expensive.

    The uncomfortable part. Backtested over decades, buying the cheapest, most-discounted stocks underperformed simply buying and holding the S&P 500. The value strategy managed roughly 0.9% a year versus about 8.6% for buy-and-hold over the same span – and it only really shone during the 2008 crash.

    Why risk matters, not just return. A trade that returns 100% but risked a 50% chance of going to zero is a losing bet if you repeat it – do it enough and you're wiped out. That's why we report the Sortino ratio, which rewards return but only punishes downside volatility. In the backtest, the value strategy's Sortino was about 0.17 versus 0.55 for buy-and-hold – roughly three times worse risk-adjusted.

    The takeaway. Value investing isn't a scam – it's that efficient markets have largely caught up to it. Use this tool to see it for yourself: appraise a stock, then backtest it and watch how often “cheap” actually beats just holding.
